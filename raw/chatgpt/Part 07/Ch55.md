Chapter 55 — Merge vs. Rebase


---

1. Purpose

This chapter defines the two fundamental mechanisms for integrating branch history into a target branch:

Merge: preserves the actual divergence and convergence of development

Rebase: rewrites history to make development appear linear


Both produce correct code. They produce very different historical artifacts, and those artifacts affect debugging, reasoning, and collaboration patterns.

This chapter is not about workflow preference — it is about what kind of history a system chooses to preserve.


---

2. Prerequisites

Assumed concepts:

Branches represent integration state, not semantic truth (Ch 50)

Commit messages are stable historical artifacts (Ch 51)

Squashing is a separate merge-time policy that can bypass both forms (Ch 52)

Force push is required for any history rewrite on shared references (Ch 54)

PRs are transient integration units, not permanent structure



---

3. Key Concepts

Integration has two orthogonal dimensions:

Structural preservation (do we keep branch topology in history?)

Linearization (do we make history appear sequential?)


Merge and rebase are different answers to both questions.


---

Merge (Preserve Graph Structure)

What it is: A merge integrates a branch into another by creating a new commit that has two parents, preserving the fact that two lines of development existed and converged.

Why it exists: To preserve the true topology of development, including parallel work, divergence, and integration points.

Options:

Fast-forward merge (linear if no divergence exists)

True merge commit (preserve branch structure explicitly)

Squash merge (collapse structure entirely — covered in Ch 52)


Trade-offs:

Preserves real development topology in history graph

Makes parallel development visible and debuggable

Introduces merge commits that can clutter linear log views

Reflects reality rather than rewriting it


When to choose each option:

Fast-forward: when no divergence exists (already linear history)

Merge commit: when branch structure is meaningful for debugging or audit

Avoiding merge commits entirely: when history readability is prioritized over topology fidelity


Common failure modes:

Excessive merge commits creating noisy graphs in long-lived branches

Misinterpreting merge commits as “complexity” rather than representation of concurrency

Teams alternating between merge and rebase, producing inconsistent history shapes

Loss of clarity when merge commits contain unrelated changes bundled from large branches


Example: The Linux kernel uses merge commits extensively. Linus Torvalds explicitly defends merge commits as the correct representation of real development history. The kernel’s history is not linear because development is not linear; multiple subsystem trees integrate over time.


---

Rebase (Linearize History)

What it is: Rebase takes a sequence of commits and replays them on top of a new base commit, rewriting their parentage so they appear as if they were created sequentially.

Why it exists: To produce a clean, linear history that removes the visible structure of branching.

Options:

Rebase local branch before review (cleanup step)

Rebase during integration (linear history enforcement)

Interactive rebase (surgical commit restructuring prior to publication)


Trade-offs:

Produces clean, linear history that is easier to read sequentially

Erases explicit representation of branching and integration points

Requires history rewriting, which interacts directly with shared-state safety rules (Ch 54)

Can make parallel development invisible in final history


When to choose each option:

Local rebase: cleaning up personal work before review (safe, recommended)

Pre-merge rebase: teams enforcing linear history in shared branches

Avoid rebase: when history integrity or shared branch safety takes priority over linearity


Common failure modes:

Rebasing shared branches, forcing downstream reconciliation (direct Ch 54 violation)

Losing context of when and how branches diverged

Creating false impression of sequential development in highly parallel systems

Developers struggling with repeated rebase conflicts in long-lived branches


Example: Atlassian’s Git documentation explicitly states the “golden rule of rebasing”: never rebase commits that have been shared with others. Once a branch is public, rebasing rewrites history that other repositories may already depend on.


---

Golden Rule of Rebasing

What it is: Never rebase commits that other people have pulled or built work on.

Why it exists: Because rebase rewrites commit identities, not just content. Downstream branches depend on those identities for merging and reconciliation.

Options:

Rebase only private branches

Rebase shared branches with coordination (rare, controlled environments)

Avoid rebase entirely in shared systems


Trade-offs:

Private rebasing improves commit quality without coordination overhead

Shared rebasing introduces cascading history conflicts across collaborators

Avoidance simplifies mental model but may leave noisy history uncleaned


When to choose each option:

Private branch cleanup: standard practice before PR

Coordinated shared rebase: controlled environments with strict synchronization windows

No rebase: systems prioritizing immutable shared history over local optimization


Common failure modes:

Developers rebasing branches others have already based work on

Force push required to publish rebased history (direct coupling with Ch 54)

Conflicts multiplying across multiple downstream branches

Loss of trust in branch stability as a shared coordination mechanism


Example: A developer rebases a shared feature branch to “clean history” and force pushes it. Other developers who branched from it now face broken histories and must manually reconcile diverging commit graphs. The cost scales with the number of dependent branches.


---

Merge vs Rebase as a History Shape Decision

What it is: A decision about whether the repository reflects actual development topology or a linearized approximation of it.

Options:

Merge-based history (graph-preserving)

Rebase-based history (linearized)

Hybrid approaches depending on branch type or integration stage


Trade-offs:

Merge preserves reality of concurrent development

Rebase improves readability and linear reasoning

Hybrid systems introduce inconsistency but can optimize for different workflows


When to choose each option:

Merge: systems where understanding parallel development matters (large-scale, multi-team systems)

Rebase: systems optimized for sequential reasoning and clean log inspection

Hybrid: most real-world systems, often implicitly rather than explicitly defined


Common failure modes:

Inconsistent history shapes across the same repository, confusing tooling and humans

Misinterpreting linear history as simpler reality than actual development structure

Overuse of rebase leading to hidden integration complexity

Overuse of merge leading to unreadable history graphs



---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

The disagreement is about what Git history is for.

One view:

History is a record of what actually happened

Merge commits are honest representations of parallel work

Rewriting history is distorting system truth


Another view:

History is a tool for reasoning, not archival truth

Linear history improves cognitive load and debugging speed

Structural accuracy is less important than readability


Both are valid:

In systems with heavy parallel development (kernels, large infrastructures), preserving structure is critical for understanding integration behavior

In product-centric systems, linear history often improves day-to-day debugging and review efficiency


The disagreement is less about correctness and more about whether history is primarily an audit log or a reasoning interface.


---

Position

Rebase and merge are not competing correctness mechanisms — they are different history models.

The stable principles are:

Never rebase shared history (golden rule)

Use rebase freely for local cleanup

Choose merge when topology matters more than linearity

Choose rebase when readability of sequential history matters more than explicit structure


Squash-based workflows (Ch 52) bypass much of this debate entirely for PR-based systems, but for systems that preserve commit structure, the merge vs rebase decision is a persistent architectural choice, not a stylistic one.

Chapter 45 — Architecture Decision Records (ADRs)

Purpose

Chapter 9 introduced the principle that architectural decisions with high reversal cost or large blast radius deserve a permanent record. Chapter 30 established that repository-level design rationale belongs in Architecture Decision Records (ADRs), not inline comments.

This chapter defines the ADR itself: its format, lifecycle, storage, and the judgment required to decide when writing one is worthwhile.

An ADR is not a design proposal. It is the historical record of an architectural decision that has been made.

Prerequisites

This chapter assumes familiarity with:

Part I, Chapter 9's reversibility × blast-radius decision framework.

Part IV, Chapter 30's distinction between implementation documentation and design rationale.

Chapter 42's discussion of issues as tracking artifacts.


The process of exploring proposed designs belongs in Chapter 46. Documentation taxonomy is covered in Part VIII.

Key Concepts

The central ideas are:

ADRs record decisions, not discussions.

Context and consequences are the most valuable parts of an ADR.

Accepted ADRs are immutable historical records.

Only significant architectural decisions deserve ADRs.

ADRs belong alongside the code they govern.



---

Architecture Decision Records

What it is

An Architecture Decision Record is a concise document that records a significant architectural decision, the circumstances under which it was made, and the consequences of making it.

Its purpose is not to convince today's team.

Its purpose is to explain today's decision to tomorrow's team.

Why it exists

Architectural reasoning is surprisingly short-lived.

A year later, engineers often remember what decision was made while forgetting why it was made.

The ADR preserves that reasoning before it disappears.

Options

Architecture Decision Record

A permanent, versioned record maintained with the code.

Institutional memory

Rely on experienced engineers remembering the rationale.

Meeting notes or chat history

Search historical conversations when questions arise.

Trade-offs

ADR

Pros:

durable rationale

searchable

version-controlled

survives personnel changes


Cons:

requires discipline

modest documentation overhead



---

Institutional memory

Pros:

zero documentation effort


Cons:

disappears over time

difficult to transfer

dependent on individuals



---

Meeting history

Pros:

captures discussion


Cons:

difficult to discover

usually lacks conclusions

poor long-term reference


When to choose each option

For architectural decisions identified in Chapter 9 as difficult to reverse or having broad system impact, record an ADR.

Routine implementation decisions do not require permanent documentation.

Common failure modes

Months later, the team remembers only that "we chose PostgreSQL" or "we adopted event sourcing."

Nobody remembers the constraints that made those choices reasonable.

The architecture is gradually re-litigated because its history has vanished.

Example

MADR and adr-tools both grew directly from Michael Nygard's ADR format, demonstrating how widely the concept has been adopted across engineering organizations.

The Canonical ADR Format

What it is

The canonical structure introduced by Michael Nygard consists of five sections:

Title

Status

Context

Decision

Consequences


Although all five matter, two sections carry most of the long-term value.

Why it exists

A consistent structure allows future readers to quickly locate the information that answers their questions.

Options

Canonical ADR format

A lightweight, consistent template.

Organization-specific template

Expanded formats with additional sections.

Trade-offs

Canonical format

Pros:

concise

widely recognized

easy to maintain


Cons:

intentionally minimal



---

Expanded templates

Pros:

capture additional organizational context

integrate with governance processes


Cons:

greater maintenance burden

encourages unnecessary documentation


When to choose each option

Begin with the canonical format.

Additional sections should only exist if they repeatedly answer questions not already covered.

Common failure modes

Organizations continuously add required sections until writing an ADR resembles completing a compliance form rather than documenting an engineering decision.

Example

ThoughtWorks helped popularize lightweight ADRs by promoting the practice in its Technology Radar, reinforcing the idea that architecture records should remain concise enough to be written consistently.


---

Context Is What Makes the Decision Understandable

What it is

The Context section records the constraints, assumptions, and facts that existed when the decision was made.

Why it exists

Architectural decisions are rarely universally correct.

They are correct within particular constraints.

Future engineers can only evaluate whether a decision still applies if they understand those original constraints.

Options

Rich context

Explain the forces influencing the decision.

Minimal context

Record only the final conclusion.

Trade-offs

Rich context

Pros:

preserves reasoning

enables future reevaluation

explains trade-offs


Cons:

requires thoughtful writing



---

Minimal context

Pros:

quick to produce


Cons:

loses historical value

encourages repeated debate


When to choose each option

Include every constraint that materially influenced the decision.

Do not attempt to document every meeting or discussion.

Common failure modes

A future team concludes that an ADR was "wrong" because the original deployment environment, business constraints, performance limits, or organizational realities are no longer visible.

The decision appears arbitrary because its assumptions have disappeared.

Example

An ADR choosing SQLite over PostgreSQL is only meaningful if the context records that deployment simplicity, embedded operation, or single-user workloads were decisive constraints.


---

Consequences Preserve Engineering History

What it is

The Consequences section records what the team knowingly accepted by making the decision.

This includes:

benefits

costs

limitations

rejected alternatives and why they were rejected


Why it exists

Chapter 9 argued that recording rejected alternatives prevents future engineers from repeatedly reconsidering the same options without historical knowledge.

The decision itself is only half the story.

The alternatives explain why that decision survived.

Options

Record consequences and rejected alternatives

Document both gains and accepted costs.

Record only the chosen solution

Treat the ADR as an announcement.

Trade-offs

Complete consequences

Pros:

discourages repeated debates

exposes accepted compromises

improves future decisions


Cons:

slightly longer documents



---

Decision only

Pros:

concise


Cons:

loses reasoning

repeated evaluation

historical ambiguity


When to choose each option

Every ADR should explain both what was gained and what was deliberately sacrificed.

Architectural decisions are trade-offs, not declarations of perfection.

Common failure modes

Engineers revisit options years later believing they are new ideas, unaware they were already evaluated under the same constraints.

Example

A team choosing a monolith over microservices should record not only the simplicity gained but also the scaling and organizational limitations knowingly accepted.


---

Accepted ADRs Are Immutable

What it is

An ADR progresses through a simple lifecycle:

Proposed

Accepted

Deprecated or Superseded


Once accepted, its contents do not change.

Why it exists

The ADR is a historical record.

Editing accepted records rewrites history.

When architectural thinking changes, the historical fact is not that the old decision became different.

The historical fact is that a new decision replaced it.

Options

Immutable accepted ADRs

New ADRs supersede previous ones.

Editable ADRs

Existing records are updated as understanding evolves.

Trade-offs

Immutable history

Pros:

preserves chronology

supports architectural archaeology

accurately records changing understanding


Cons:

multiple ADRs may discuss related topics



---

Editable history

Pros:

fewer documents

current information in one place


Cons:

destroys historical context

obscures decision evolution

makes past reasoning impossible to reconstruct


When to choose each option

Treat accepted ADRs as immutable.

If the architecture changes, create a new ADR referencing the previous record and explaining why circumstances have changed.

Common failure modes

Teams silently modify accepted ADRs until the document reflects today's architecture rather than yesterday's decision.

Future readers lose precisely the information the ADR was intended to preserve.

Example

Many repositories organize ADRs as numbered records where later documents explicitly supersede earlier ones rather than editing them in place.


---

When an ADR Is Warranted

What it is

Not every engineering decision deserves permanent documentation.

Chapter 9 already established the decision framework.

This chapter applies it.

Why it exists

Writing ADRs consumes engineering effort.

Their value depends on preserving decisions that future engineers are genuinely likely to revisit.

Options

Record high-impact architectural decisions

Document decisions that are expensive to reverse or broadly affect the system.

Record every technical decision

Treat ADRs as a complete engineering journal.

Trade-offs

Selective ADRs

Pros:

high signal

manageable collection

frequently consulted


Cons:

requires judgment



---

ADR for everything

Pros:

comprehensive history


Cons:

overwhelming volume

difficult navigation

important decisions become harder to find


When to choose each option

Architectural decisions that are both difficult to reverse and have broad blast radius should almost always receive an ADR.

Examples include:

persistence technology

distributed communication model

deployment architecture

authentication strategy

service decomposition


Local implementation choices, naming decisions, internal class organization, or routine library usage generally do not.

Common failure modes

ADR inflation.

As more trivial decisions receive ADRs, engineers stop consulting the collection altogether because locating important records becomes difficult.


---

Keep ADRs With the Code

What it is

ADRs belong inside the repository they describe.

The common convention is a directory such as:

docs/adr/

docs/decisions/


Versioned alongside the code.

Why it exists

Architectural decisions evolve with software.

Separating them into external systems encourages divergence.

Options

Repository-local ADRs

Versioned with implementation.

Wiki or documentation portal

Maintained separately.

Trade-offs

Repository storage

Pros:

version alignment

easier review

travels with forks

naturally maintained


Cons:

duplicated across repositories where appropriate



---

External wiki

Pros:

centralized documentation


Cons:

drift

uncertain ownership

disconnected version history


When to choose each option

Store ADRs with the code they govern.

If the repository changes, the architectural history should change with it.

Common failure modes

The architecture evolves through code while the wiki quietly becomes obsolete.

Engineers stop trusting the documentation because it no longer matches reality.

Example

The docs/adr/ directory has become the de facto convention across many open-source and commercial projects because version control naturally keeps architectural history synchronized with implementation.


---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

Most disagreement is not about the value of documenting major architectural decisions. It is about where to draw the threshold.

Some teams write ADRs for nearly every technical choice, believing comprehensive documentation is always beneficial.

Others reserve ADRs for only the most consequential architectural commitments, arguing that excessive documentation becomes invisible through sheer volume.

The stronger position is generally the second. The purpose of an ADR is not to maximize documentation. It is to preserve decisions whose future reconsideration would otherwise require rediscovering lost reasoning.

An ADR should answer a question that someone is realistically likely to ask years later: Why did we choose this architecture instead of the alternatives? If the answer would still matter after the original engineers have left, the decision probably deserves an ADR. If not, ordinary code, tests, and reviews are usually sufficient.

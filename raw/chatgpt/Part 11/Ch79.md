Chapter 79 — Threat Modeling

Purpose

Security controls are responses to threats. Before deciding how to authenticate users, validate input, manage secrets, or secure the software supply chain, an engineer must first understand what is being protected, from whom, and where the opportunities for compromise exist.

This chapter establishes the vocabulary and methodology used throughout Part XI:

Assets

Adversaries

Trust boundaries

Attack surface


It also introduces a structured approach to identifying threats before implementation begins. Every later chapter assumes this framework rather than re-establishing it.

Security Begins with Understanding the System

Many security failures are not caused by weak cryptography or incorrect code.

They arise because engineers protected the wrong thing.

A team may spend months strengthening password storage while leaving an administrative API unintentionally exposed to the Internet.

Another may implement sophisticated authorization while allowing sensitive internal topology to leak through diagnostic metadata.

Threat modeling exists to prevent this class of mistake.

Its purpose is not to predict every attack.

Its purpose is to systematically identify where attacks are possible before implementation begins.


---

Decision / Principle — Identify Assets First

What it is

An asset is anything whose compromise would matter.

Assets include far more than databases or customer records.

They also include:

Credentials

Cryptographic keys

Build infrastructure

Source code

Configuration

Availability

Reputation

Customer trust


Anything whose loss changes the system's security posture is an asset.


---

Why it exists

Security controls are expensive.

Protecting every component equally is impossible.

Threat modeling begins by determining what actually deserves protection.

The value of a control depends on the value of the asset it protects.

This directly reflects Chapter 9's framing.

High-blast-radius, low-reversibility assets deserve significantly more attention than assets whose compromise is easily reversed.


---

Options

Assets may be classified by:

Business value

Operational importance

Legal or regulatory impact

Recovery difficulty

Blast radius



---

Trade-offs

Advantages of explicit asset identification:

Focuses engineering effort

Clarifies priorities

Avoids protecting unimportant components while overlooking critical ones


Costs:

Requires early design effort

Must be maintained as systems evolve

May reveal uncomfortable architectural weaknesses



---

When to choose each option

Treat as critical assets anything whose compromise would:

expose customer data,

enable privilege escalation,

compromise software releases,

allow persistent unauthorized access,

or significantly expand blast radius.


Not every configuration file deserves the same protection as a production signing key.


---

Common failure modes

Treating every component as equally valuable

Protecting user passwords while overlooking deployment credentials

Ignoring operational infrastructure

Assuming "internal" automatically means "safe"



---

Example

A code-signing key used to publish software often represents a higher-value asset than the application source code itself.

Source code may reveal implementation details.

A compromised signing key allows attackers to distribute malicious software as though it originated from the legitimate publisher.


---

Decision / Principle — Identify Adversaries Explicitly

What it is

An adversary is any party that benefits from compromising an asset.

Threat modeling asks not only who the adversary is, but also what capabilities they realistically possess.


---

Why it exists

Different adversaries present different risks.

A casual Internet scanner does not possess the same resources as an organized criminal group.

A malicious insider differs fundamentally from an external attacker.

Security decisions should reflect realistic capabilities rather than vague assumptions about "hackers."


---

Options

Typical adversaries include:

Anonymous Internet attackers

Authenticated users

Malicious insiders

Compromised third parties

Automated malware

Nation-state actors



---

Trade-offs

Advantages:

Produces realistic security requirements

Prevents overengineering

Prevents underestimating likely attacks


Costs:

Requires assumptions

Threats evolve over time

Excessively broad models become impractical



---

When to choose each option

Model adversaries according to the actual deployment environment.

Internal enterprise software rarely requires the same assumptions as public cloud infrastructure.

Conversely, Internet-facing systems should never assume benign external users.


---

Common failure modes

Modeling only external attackers

Ignoring compromised internal accounts

Assuming trusted partners remain trustworthy indefinitely

Designing against hypothetical attackers while overlooking common ones



---

Example

Many ransomware attacks begin not with sophisticated exploitation but with stolen employee credentials.

The adversary initially appears indistinguishable from a legitimate user.


---

Decision / Principle — Trust Boundaries Define Security Architecture

What it is

A trust boundary exists wherever data or control moves between components operating under different trust assumptions.

Every boundary changes what assumptions remain valid.


---

Why it exists

Security problems frequently arise at boundaries rather than within individual components.

Crossing a trust boundary means accepting information produced elsewhere.

Every such transition deserves explicit examination.

Chapter 24 introduced both the confused deputy problem and zero-trust architecture as examples of trust-boundary reasoning.

Those concepts are specific applications of the more general methodology established here.


---

Options

Typical trust boundaries include:

Internet to application

Browser to server

Service to service

Customer to administrator

Build system to deployment environment

Third-party vendor to internal infrastructure



---

Trade-offs

Advantages:

Makes hidden assumptions visible

Encourages explicit security decisions

Produces clearer system architecture


Costs:

Additional design work

More diagrams and documentation

Requires maintaining architectural understanding



---

When to choose each option

Every transition between different administrative domains, privilege levels, or ownership boundaries should be treated as a trust boundary until demonstrated otherwise.


---

Common failure modes

Assuming internal networks are trusted

Treating authenticated users as fully trusted

Forgetting vendor integrations

Allowing debugging interfaces to cross security boundaries unnoticed



---

Example

An administrative API accessible only from an internal network still crosses a trust boundary.

A compromised internal workstation changes the security assumptions immediately.


---

Decision / Principle — Enumerate the Attack Surface

What it is

The attack surface is everything reachable from outside the current trust boundary.

Every externally reachable interface represents another opportunity for misuse.


---

Why it exists

Unreachable code cannot be attacked.

Reachable code can.

Threat modeling therefore asks a simple question repeatedly:

"What can an external party reach from here?"

Reducing attack surface often improves security before individual defensive mechanisms are even considered.


---

Options

Attack surface includes:

Network APIs

Administrative interfaces

Configuration endpoints

File upload paths

Build pipelines

Metadata

Diagnostic endpoints



---

Trade-offs

Advantages of explicit enumeration:

Reveals forgotten interfaces

Clarifies exposure

Supports systematic review


Costs:

Architectural documentation must remain current

Hidden interfaces are easily overlooked



---

When to choose each option

Inventory every externally reachable interface before considering how it should be protected.

Threat modeling concerns identifying exposure rather than selecting mitigations.

Those mitigations are the subject of later chapters.


---

Common failure modes

Forgotten debug endpoints

Legacy APIs remaining publicly accessible

Exposed administrative interfaces

Internal metadata unintentionally crossing trust boundaries



---

Example

Chapter 72 deferred one such example.

Distributed trace context crossing an untrusted boundary becomes part of the attack surface.

Trace identifiers, service names, infrastructure topology, deployment conventions, or internal routing information may reveal architectural details never intended for external users.

The tracing mechanism itself is not the problem.

Crossing the trust boundary without considering what information accompanies it is.


---

Decision / Principle — Use a Structured Threat Modeling Method

What it is

Threat modeling should follow a repeatable methodology rather than relying on intuition.

One of the most widely used frameworks is STRIDE, which examines each trust boundary by asking whether it enables:

Spoofing

Tampering

Repudiation

Information Disclosure

Denial of Service

Elevation of Privilege


The framework is valuable because it makes omissions less likely.


---

Why it exists

"Think like an attacker" is useful advice.

It is not a process.

Engineers naturally overlook unfamiliar attack categories.

Structured checklists reduce reliance on individual experience.


---

Options

Informal brainstorming

STRIDE

Organization-specific threat catalogs

Industry threat libraries



---

Trade-offs

Advantages of structured methods:

Repeatable

Teachable

Easier to review

Less dependent on individual expertise


Costs:

Additional design effort

May identify threats requiring later analysis

Can become a paperwork exercise if applied mechanically



---

When to choose each option

For most software systems, STRIDE provides an effective design review framework because it systematically examines every trust boundary instead of relying on memory or intuition.


---

Common failure modes

Treating STRIDE as a compliance checklist

Applying categories without understanding the system

Reviewing components rather than trust boundaries

Performing threat modeling only after implementation



---

Example

Microsoft popularized STRIDE specifically as a structured methodology for examining system designs before implementation, and it remains widely used across the industry.


---

Decision / Principle — Threat Modeling Is a Design Activity

What it is

Threat modeling belongs during system design.

It is not primarily an incident-response activity.


---

Why it exists

Every architectural decision constrains future security options.

Once interfaces, protocols, trust boundaries, and deployment models become established, correcting fundamental design flaws becomes progressively more expensive.

This is Principle 10 applied to security.

Chapter 46 argued that specification review is cheaper than implementation review.

Threat modeling extends the same reasoning.

Finding a trust-boundary mistake before code exists is dramatically less expensive than discovering it after deployment.


---

Options

Design-time threat modeling

Implementation review

Post-incident analysis


Each remains valuable.

Only the first prevents architectural mistakes before they become code.


---

Trade-offs

Advantages:

Lower correction cost

Better architecture

Fewer security-driven redesigns

Clearer documentation


Costs:

Up-front design effort

Some identified risks never materialize


The asymmetry strongly favors early analysis because redesigning deployed security architecture is rarely inexpensive.


---

When to choose each option

Perform threat modeling whenever:

introducing new trust boundaries,

exposing new external interfaces,

integrating third-party systems,

or making significant architectural changes.


Post-incident reviews remain valuable, but they should refine future threat models rather than replace them.


---

Common failure modes

Treating threat modeling as compliance documentation

Waiting until penetration testing

Performing security review after interfaces become fixed

Assuming implementation alone determines security



---

Example

A design review identifying an unnecessarily Internet-accessible administrative endpoint before implementation typically requires changing a diagram.

Finding the same exposure after deployment may require architectural redesign, migration planning, customer communication, and operational downtime.


---

Case Study — The Target Data Breach

The 2013 breach of Target illustrates why trust boundaries deserve careful attention.

Attackers did not initially compromise Target's customer payment systems directly.

They first obtained credentials belonging to a third-party HVAC vendor with legitimate access to portions of Target's network.

That comparatively narrow trust relationship became the entry point into a much larger environment.

The vendor credentials were not the ultimate target.

They were the path across a trust boundary.

The incident demonstrates that seemingly low-value integrations can possess disproportionately large blast radius when trust boundaries are poorly understood.

Threat modeling exists to identify exactly these indirect paths before they become attack paths.


---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

Experienced engineers rarely disagree about whether threat modeling is useful.

They disagree about how formal it should be.

One approach emphasizes lightweight design reviews supported by engineering judgment.

Another favors structured methodologies, documented threat models, and repeatable review processes.

Smaller systems often benefit from lighter processes because their trust boundaries remain relatively simple.

Large platforms, critical infrastructure, and long-lived systems benefit from greater formality because the cost of overlooking a trust boundary grows with system complexity.

The important distinction is not informal versus formal.

It is proactive versus reactive.

Threat modeling that occurs after implementation has already lost much of its economic advantage.

Chapter 82 — Authentication vs. Authorization

Purpose

Nearly every system must answer two independent security questions:

1. Who is making this request?


2. What is that caller allowed to do?



These questions are often discussed together, but they solve different problems and fail in different ways.

This chapter distinguishes authentication from authorization, examines the dominant mechanisms used to implement each, and provides guidance on choosing among them. It assumes Chapter 24 has already established where authorization logic belongs architecturally. The focus here is on the mechanics of authentication and authorization themselves.

Authentication and Authorization Are Different Problems

Authentication answers:

> Who is this caller?



Authorization answers:

> What is this authenticated caller allowed to do?



Neither question implies the other.

A system may authenticate a caller perfectly while granting excessive permissions.

Conversely, a perfectly designed authorization policy cannot compensate for incorrectly identifying the caller.

Confusing these responsibilities often produces security architectures that appear correct while failing in practice.


---

Decision / Principle — Separate Identity from Permission

What it is

Authentication establishes identity.

Authorization evaluates permissions associated with that identity.

They are independent decisions that happen in sequence.


---

Why it exists

Identity alone provides no authority.

Knowing a caller is Alice does not imply Alice should modify payroll records.

Likewise, a carefully designed authorization policy is meaningless if the system mistakes Mallory for Alice.

Separating the concerns simplifies reasoning and allows each mechanism to evolve independently.


---

Options

Authentication mechanisms:

Passwords

Client certificates

Hardware security keys

Federated identity providers


Authorization mechanisms:

RBAC

ABAC

Custom policy engines



---

Trade-offs

Separating authentication and authorization:

Advantages

Clear responsibilities

Simpler auditing

Independent evolution

Reduced coupling


Costs

Additional architectural components

More explicit identity propagation

Multiple security decisions per request


Combining both responsibilities into one mechanism generally produces systems that are harder to extend and audit.


---

When to choose each option

Always authenticate before authorizing.

Authorization decisions should operate on verified identities rather than unauthenticated request metadata.


---

Common failure modes

Treating authentication as sufficient authorization

Assuming administrators may perform every action

Authorizing requests before identity is established

Using usernames directly as permission checks



---

Example

An employee successfully authenticating to a corporate portal should still receive only the permissions associated with their assigned responsibilities rather than unrestricted access to every internal system.

Decision / Principle — Choose Between Sessions and Tokens Deliberately

What it is

The two dominant authentication mechanisms are server-managed sessions and cryptographically signed tokens.

A session stores authoritative authentication state on the server.

The client typically presents a session identifier using a cookie.

A token carries authenticated identity information directly, commonly as a signed JSON Web Token (JWT).


---

Why it exists

Both approaches establish authenticated identity.

They differ primarily in where authentication state lives.

Sessions centralize state on the server.

Tokens distribute identity information to clients while protecting integrity through cryptographic signatures.


---

Options

Server-side sessions

Signed JWTs

Opaque bearer tokens with server lookup



---

Trade-offs

Server-side sessions

Advantages:

Immediate revocation

Centralized control

Simpler logout semantics

Server remains authoritative


Costs:

Server-side storage

State lookup on every request

Horizontal scaling requires shared session storage or affinity


Signed tokens

Advantages:

Stateless verification

No session lookup

Easy distribution across services

Excellent scalability


Costs:

Revocation is difficult before expiration

Token lifetime becomes a security decision

Additional server-side state is required for immediate revocation


The apparent simplicity of stateless tokens often hides the operational complexity of revocation.


---

When to choose each option

Prefer sessions when immediate revocation and centralized control are important.

Prefer signed tokens when services must authenticate requests without consulting a shared session store on every request.

Neither approach is universally superior.

The appropriate choice depends primarily on revocation requirements and operational architecture.


---

Common failure modes

Extremely long-lived tokens

Assuming JWTs are automatically revocable

Treating signed tokens as encrypted

Storing excessive information inside tokens



---

Example

Most traditional web applications authenticate users through server-managed sessions.

Many modern APIs authenticate requests using signed JWTs verified independently by multiple services.


---

Decision / Principle — Use OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect for Delegated Identity

What it is

OAuth 2.0 provides a framework for delegated authorization.

OpenID Connect (OIDC) extends OAuth 2.0 to provide standardized authentication and identity information.

Together they form the dominant identity architecture for modern Internet services.


---

Why it exists

Applications frequently need limited access to resources without learning a user's primary credential.

Delegation avoids sharing passwords between systems.

Instead, users authenticate with an identity provider, which issues credentials permitting limited access to specific resources.

This separation significantly reduces credential exposure.


---

Options

Direct username/password authentication

OAuth 2.0 delegation

OpenID Connect federated authentication



---

Trade-offs

OAuth/OIDC:

Advantages

Federated identity

Delegated access

Reduced password sharing

Mature ecosystem

Broad interoperability


Costs:

Protocol complexity

Multiple interacting components

Careful implementation required

Additional operational infrastructure


The complexity largely reflects the breadth of problems these protocols solve.


---

When to choose each option

Use OAuth 2.0 whenever one service must access another on behalf of a user without learning the user's primary credential.

Use OpenID Connect whenever standardized authentication and identity information are also required.


---

Common failure modes

Misconfigured redirect URIs

Excessively broad scopes

Long-lived access tokens

Incorrect trust relationships between identity providers and applications



---

Example

Most major identity providers—including Microsoft Entra ID, Google Identity, and Okta—implement OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect as their primary authentication architecture.

Decision / Principle — Match Authorization Policy to Organizational Complexity

What it is

Once identity has been established, the system must determine whether the requested action is permitted.

The two dominant policy models are Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) and Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC).


---

Why it exists

Different organizations express permissions differently.

Some naturally organize around stable job roles.

Others require context-sensitive decisions that depend on runtime information.

No single authorization model fits both equally well.


---

Options

RBAC

ABAC

Hybrid approaches



---

Trade-offs

RBAC

Advantages:

Simple

Easy to audit

Stable permission assignments

Clear organizational mapping


Costs:

Role explosion as systems grow

Poor fit for contextual policies

Difficult to express exceptions cleanly


ABAC

Advantages:

Flexible

Expressive

Handles contextual decisions

Reduces dependence on rigid role hierarchies


Costs:

Harder to understand

More difficult to audit

Larger policy surface

Greater operational complexity


RBAC optimizes for simplicity.

ABAC optimizes for expressive power.


---

When to choose each option

Choose RBAC when permissions naturally correspond to a relatively small number of stable organizational roles.

Choose ABAC when authorization depends on runtime characteristics such as:

resource ownership,

request location,

time,

project membership,

environmental context,

or combinations of these factors.


Hybrid systems are common, using RBAC for coarse permissions and ABAC for finer-grained constraints.


---

Common failure modes

Creating hundreds of narrowly defined roles

Encoding contextual decisions inside role names

Overly complex attribute policies that become impossible to audit

Granting permanent permissions for temporary situations



---

Example

Kubernetes implements RBAC by binding users, groups, or service accounts to roles that define permitted operations on Kubernetes resources.

The model remains understandable because permissions are grouped into relatively stable administrative roles.


---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

Authentication technologies tend to converge on industry standards.

Authorization rarely does.

Some organizations strongly prefer RBAC because policies remain understandable and auditable.

Others find RBAC too rigid for modern systems where permissions depend heavily on resource ownership, organizational relationships, geographic location, or runtime context.

The disagreement reflects competing optimization targets.

RBAC minimizes policy complexity.

ABAC minimizes exceptions to policy.

Neither eliminates complexity.

They move it to different places.


---

Real Systems

JSON Web Tokens (JWTs)

JWTs are the most common format for signed authentication tokens.

A JWT allows services to verify that identity information has not been altered without consulting a central session store.

Importantly, signing guarantees integrity—not confidentiality.

Anyone possessing the token can typically read its contents unless additional encryption is applied.


---

OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect

OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect underpin authentication for countless modern applications.

Rather than distributing passwords among many services, they centralize identity while allowing controlled delegation through standardized protocols.


---

Kubernetes RBAC

Kubernetes authorizes requests by associating subjects with roles and roles with permissions on cluster resources.

Its design demonstrates RBAC applied at infrastructure scale while remaining comparatively straightforward to audit.

Looking Ahead

Authentication establishes identity.

Authorization determines permissions.

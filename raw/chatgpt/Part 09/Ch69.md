Chapter 69 — Logging: What to Log and at What Level

Purpose

Logging is the oldest and most widely deployed observability mechanism in software systems. Every operating system, application server, database, and distributed service emits logs because every system eventually needs to answer a simple question:

"What happened?"

This chapter establishes how to answer that question without creating an unmanageable volume of information. Like comments (Chapter 30) and documentation (Chapter 64), logs are information artifacts that incur permanent maintenance costs. Every log entry must therefore justify its existence by supporting a future operational decision.

This chapter focuses only on what belongs in a log and how to classify it. The broader question of when a log is preferable to a metric or a distributed trace is deferred to Chapter 70.

Prerequisites

This chapter assumes familiarity with:

Chapter 02's discussion of complexity as an ongoing maintenance cost.

Principle 6: every engineering activity must justify its operational overhead.

Chapter 21's definition of correlation IDs.

Chapter 64's discussion of documentation coverage versus documentation usefulness.



---

Key Concepts

The central ideas introduced here are:

Logs exist to support future investigation, not present emotion.

Every log line has an ongoing storage, indexing, and query cost.

Log levels classify operational importance, not developer frustration.

Structured logs scale; free-text logs do not.

Correlation IDs belong in every request-scoped log.

Logging is part of a system's security surface.



---

Logging Exists to Preserve Decisions, Not Events

What it is

A useful log records information that enables someone to make a decision after an event has already occurred.

Why it exists

Most production failures are investigated after the fact.

The request has already failed.

The customer has already reported a bug.

The deployment has already completed.

The engineer can no longer attach a debugger or inspect program state directly.

The log exists to preserve enough information that the missing context can be reconstructed.

Options

1. Log every event.


2. Log only operationally meaningful events.


3. Log almost nothing.



Trade-offs

Log everything

Pros

Maximum historical information.

Easy for developers to add logging.


Cons

Massive infrastructure cost.

Difficult investigations due to information overload.

Increased risk of exposing sensitive information.

Slower queries.



---

Log only operationally meaningful events

Pros

Higher signal-to-noise ratio.

Lower storage cost.

Faster investigations.

Easier retention management.


Cons

Requires engineering judgment.

Missing information occasionally requires new logging.



---

Log almost nothing

Pros

Minimal runtime overhead.

Minimal storage.


Cons

Extremely difficult production debugging.

Incidents become dependent on reproduction.


When to choose each option

Most production systems should prefer operationally meaningful logging.

Very high-throughput systems may intentionally reduce logging volume further.

Logging everything is rarely justified outside temporary debugging or controlled development environments.

Common failure modes

The most common failure is treating logs as insurance.

Developers reason that storage is cheap and additional context might someday be useful.

The result resembles the documentation anti-pattern discussed in Chapter 64: coverage replaces usefulness as the optimization target.

Eventually nobody reads the majority of the data being collected.

The organization pays continuous costs for hypothetical future value that never materializes.

Example

Large internet companies routinely reduce log volume through sampling, aggregation, and careful event selection because ingestion, indexing, and long-term storage become major operational expenses once systems produce millions of events per second.


---

Log What Someone Can Act Upon

What it is

A useful test for whether something belongs in a log is:

> Who will read this, and what decision will they make because it exists?



If neither question has an answer, the log entry likely does not belong.

Why it exists

Developers often ask:

> "Is this interesting?"



Operations instead asks:

> "Would this change what I conclude?"



Those are different questions.

Options

Log:

State transitions

Failures

Configuration changes

External interactions

Security-relevant events

Lifecycle events


Avoid logging:

Obvious control flow

Every function entry

Every successful internal helper call

Variable dumps with no future diagnostic value


Trade-offs

Recording meaningful state transitions creates concise investigative timelines.

Recording every internal step creates large narratives that rarely survive first contact with production scale.

When to choose each option

Log whenever losing the information would make post-incident reasoning materially harder.

Do not log information that can already be reconstructed from existing logs.

Common failure modes

Engineers frequently log implementation details instead of system behavior.

A future maintainer rarely needs to know that function ValidateInput() executed.

They often need to know:

validation failed

which validation rule failed

which request failed

which customer or tenant was affected

which correlation ID connects this event to the rest of the request


Example

A payment service should log:

payment authorized

authorization declined

gateway timeout

fraud rule triggered


It should not log every helper function involved in calculating those outcomes.

Log Levels Are Operational Classifications

What it is

Log levels classify how an event should be treated during investigation.

They do not measure how strongly the programmer felt while writing the code.

Why it exists

Without levels, filtering becomes impossible.

The long-established model originates from the severity taxonomy used by syslog, which established the convention that operational events can be classified according to their significance rather than simply listed chronologically.

Options

At minimum:

DEBUG

INFO

WARN

ERROR


Many systems additionally include TRACE, FATAL, or CRITICAL.

Trade-offs

DEBUG

Pros

Rich diagnostic information.

Excellent during development.


Cons

Excessive production volume.



---

INFO

Pros

Captures important lifecycle events.

Useful operational history.


Cons

Can become noisy if overused.



---

WARN

Pros

Highlights unexpected conditions.

Indicates degraded behavior without outright failure.


Cons

Frequently abused for normal situations.



---

ERROR

Pros

Identifies failed operations requiring investigation.


Cons

Loses meaning if expected failures are logged as errors.


When to choose each option

Choose the level according to operational significance.

An event should be an ERROR when someone investigating the service would conclude:

> "This operation failed."



Not:

> "This surprised me while writing the code."



Likewise, an expected retryable network timeout may be WARN in one service but DEBUG in another if automatic retries completely mask the failure from users.

The same underlying event may legitimately receive different classifications in different systems because different operators are expected to respond.

Common failure modes

Everything becomes WARN.

Everything becomes ERROR.

Eventually operators stop trusting severity entirely.

Filtering ceases to work because every event appears equally important.

Example

A failed user login due to an incorrect password is usually INFO rather than ERROR.

The authentication system behaved exactly as designed.

A failure to reach the authentication database, however, is an ERROR because the service failed to perform its intended function.


---

Structured Logging Versus Free-Text Logging

What it is

Structured logging records stable machine-readable fields alongside the human-readable message.

Typical fields include:

timestamp

level

service

hostname

correlation_id

request_id

user_id (when appropriate)

operation

status

duration_ms


Often these are emitted as JSON.

Why it exists

Humans read messages.

Machines search fields.

Production observability increasingly depends on machines.

Options

Free-text logging

Payment failed for customer 48291 after timeout

Structured logging

{
  "level": "ERROR",
  "event": "payment_failed",
  "customer_id": 48291,
  "reason": "timeout",
  "correlation_id": "...",
  "duration_ms": 3012
}

Trade-offs

Free-text

Pros

Easy to write.

Easy to read while tailing a local log.


Cons

Difficult to search consistently.

Difficult to aggregate.

Difficult to analyze automatically.

Requires brittle text parsing.



---

Structured logging

Pros

Queryable.

Machine-readable.

Stable across software versions.

Enables aggregation and dashboards.

Supports large centralized logging systems such as JSON pipelines feeding Elasticsearch- or ELK-style backends.


Cons

Slightly more effort.

Requires field naming discipline.

Poor schema design creates inconsistency.


When to choose each option

Free-text remains acceptable for local development tools or temporary debugging.

Production services should strongly prefer structured logging.

Once logs are aggregated across dozens or hundreds of services, machine-readable fields become essential.

Common failure modes

Teams add structured fields but continue embedding critical information only inside the message string.

Queries then become fragile regular expressions instead of simple field lookups.

Another common failure is unstable field naming:

user
userId
uid
customer
customer_id

These represent the same concept differently, making organization-wide queries unnecessarily difficult.

Example

Modern centralized logging platforms commonly ingest JSON logs because structured fields can be indexed directly instead of extracted later through expensive parsing.

Correlation IDs Belong in Every Request Log

What it is

Every request-scoped log entry should include the correlation ID introduced in Chapter 21.

Why it exists

A production request rarely generates only one log entry.

It may produce hundreds.

Without a stable identifier, reconstructing the request becomes largely manual.

The correlation ID provides the minimum linkage necessary before Chapter 72 expands this idea into full distributed tracing.

Options

No correlation identifier.

Session identifier only.

Correlation ID on every request-scoped log.


Trade-offs

Including the correlation ID adds a small amount of storage to every log entry.

The investigative value overwhelmingly exceeds this cost because a complete request history becomes searchable with a single query.

When to choose each option

Any request that crosses process boundaries should carry a correlation ID.

Background jobs should generate their own execution identifier.

Common failure modes

The application generates a correlation ID but only logs it during request startup.

Subsequent log entries omit it.

Investigators can no longer reconstruct the execution timeline.

Example

Many HTTP services propagate a request identifier across downstream services, allowing every participating component to emit independently searchable logs even before distributed tracing infrastructure is available.


---

Logging Has Real Operational Costs

What it is

Logs consume infrastructure resources throughout their lifecycle.

They must be transmitted, stored, replicated, indexed, retained, queried, and eventually deleted.

Why it exists

Logging is frequently treated as "free."

It is not.

Every retained byte has recurring operational cost.

Options

High-volume logging.

Moderate targeted logging.

Aggressive sampling and filtering.


Trade-offs

Higher volume improves the probability that useful information exists.

Higher volume also increases:

ingestion costs

storage costs

indexing costs

query latency

retention complexity


These costs grow continuously rather than only during incidents.

When to choose each option

Increase logging only when the expected reduction in investigation time justifies the recurring operational expense.

Temporary diagnostic logging during incident response is often justified.

Permanent diagnostic logging rarely is.

Common failure modes

Logging expands continuously because adding information is easier than removing it.

Years later, organizations discover that most indexed fields are never queried.

The logging system has become an expensive archive rather than an operational tool.

Example

Large centralized logging platforms frequently become one of the most expensive components of observability infrastructure because indexing and long-term retention scale with data volume rather than application complexity.


---

Logging Has Security Consequences

What it is

Logs create additional copies of application data.

Those copies deserve the same security consideration as the original system.

Why it exists

Developers often think only about application databases when considering sensitive information.

Logs frequently become a second, less carefully audited data store.

Options

Log:

identifiers

operational context

error classifications


Avoid logging:

passwords

authentication tokens

cryptographic secrets

personal data unless operationally required


Detailed guidance on secrets management belongs to Chapter 83.

Trade-offs

More context improves investigations.

More context also increases exposure if logging infrastructure is compromised or broadly accessible.

When to choose each option

Record the minimum sensitive information necessary for operational diagnosis.

Prefer identifiers over full payloads whenever possible.

Common failure modes

Entire HTTP requests are logged without review.

Authorization headers.

Cookies.

Session tokens.

Personally identifiable information.

These become searchable across the organization's logging infrastructure.

Example

The widely exploited Log4Shell (CVE-2021-44228) vulnerability demonstrated that logging infrastructure is itself part of the application's attack surface. Logging libraries are not passive utilities; what they record, how they process logged data, and how downstream systems interpret it all have security implications. Treat logging as production infrastructure, not debugging convenience.


---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

Experienced engineers rarely disagree about whether logging is useful.

They disagree about how much is enough.

One philosophy argues that missing information during a production incident is far more expensive than storing additional logs.

The opposing philosophy argues that excessive logging creates permanent operational cost, slower investigations, increased security exposure, and a lower signal-to-noise ratio.

Both positions arise from legitimate operational experience.

Teams that have repeatedly failed to diagnose incidents tend to favor more logging.

Teams operating very large fleets often discover that indiscriminate logging becomes one of the largest recurring infrastructure expenses and one of the biggest obstacles to efficient investigation.

The common resolution is not to maximize or minimize logging volume, but to maximize the amount of operationally useful information per log entry.

That aligns logging with the optimization principle established throughout this handbook: collect information because it enables better decisions, not because collecting information feels inherently safer.


---

Summary

Logging records the historical evidence needed to reconstruct system behavior after direct observation is no longer possible. Its value is determined by the decisions it enables, not by the quantity of data collected.

The most effective logs are structured, consistently classified, and intentionally selective. Stable machine-readable fields make large-scale querying practical, correlation IDs tie together the events of a single request, and severity levels communicate operational significance rather than developer sentiment. Every additional log entry carries ongoing costs in ingestion, storage, indexing, query performance, and potential security exposure, so "log everything" is no more defensible than documenting everything or commenting every line of code.

The next chapter broadens the perspective from a single observability signal to the complete observability toolbox, examining when logs are the appropriate signal and when a metric or distributed trace provides a better answer to the operational question being asked.

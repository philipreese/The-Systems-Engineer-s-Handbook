Chapter 71 — Alerting: Signal vs. Noise

Purpose

Observability exists to make system behavior visible. Alerting exists to decide when that visibility justifies interrupting a human being.

That distinction matters. Most observations should never become alerts, and most alerts should never become pages. Every alert consumes attention, interrupts work, and often disturbs someone outside normal working hours. Like every engineering process discussed throughout this handbook, alerting must justify its cost.

This chapter establishes the decision layer that sits above the signals introduced in Chapters 69 and 70. It answers a single question:

Which conditions are important enough to notify a human immediately?

What the responder does after receiving that notification belongs to the runbooks discussed in Chapter 68. What operational objective those alerts ultimately protect belongs to Chapter 73.

Key Concepts

The central ideas introduced here are:

An alert is a request for human attention.

Human attention is a scarce operational resource.

Every page should have a clear next action.

Dashboards, tickets, and pages serve different purposes.

Alert fatigue is the primary failure mode of monitoring.

Alert on user-visible symptoms rather than speculative internal causes.



---

An Alert Is a Decision to Interrupt Someone

What it is

An alert is an automated decision that a human should stop what they are doing and investigate the current system state.

It is not simply a notification.

Why it exists

Software detects conditions much faster than humans.

Humans provide judgment when automated recovery is insufficient.

An alert therefore exists only when automation alone cannot safely resolve the situation.

Options

Systems can:

record the event

display it on a dashboard

create a ticket

notify someone asynchronously

page an on-call responder


Each represents an increasing cost in human attention.

Trade-offs

Page immediately

Pros

Fast response.

Reduced outage duration.

Appropriate for user-impacting failures.


Cons

Interrupts work or sleep.

High operational cost.

Contributes to fatigue if overused.



---

Create a ticket

Pros

Preserves visibility.

Allows planned investigation.


Cons

Slower response.

Unsuitable for urgent incidents.



---

Dashboard only

Pros

No interruption.

Continuous operational awareness.


Cons

Requires active monitoring.

Problems may go unnoticed until someone looks.


When to choose each option

Page only when delaying human intervention would materially worsen the outcome.

Everything else should be handled through less disruptive mechanisms.

Common failure modes

Organizations often confuse "important" with "urgent."

Many operational conditions deserve investigation.

Very few deserve waking someone at 3:00 a.m.

Example

A failed nightly report generation may deserve a ticket.

A payment system returning errors to customers deserves a page.


---

The Actionability Test

What it is

An alert should exist only if receiving it implies a clear next action.

Ideally, that action is documented in a runbook, as described in Chapter 68.

Why it exists

An alert without an obvious response creates work without creating value.

It informs someone that something happened while providing no guidance about what should happen next.

Options

Page on conditions that have:

an expected responder

a known investigation path

a documented recovery procedure


Avoid paging for conditions that merely provide interesting information.

Trade-offs

Restricting alerts to actionable conditions reduces total coverage but dramatically increases the usefulness of every alert that remains.

When to choose each option

Ask:

> If this alert fires, what exactly should the responder do?



If no answer exists, the alert probably should not exist either.

Common failure modes

Teams add alerts faster than they develop operational procedures.

Eventually responders receive notifications that nobody understands or owns.

These alerts are acknowledged, silenced, or ignored rather than investigated.

Example

An alert reading:

> Database replication lag exceeded five seconds.



is actionable if the team has documented investigation and recovery steps.

An alert reading:

> Cache efficiency decreased by 2%.



may not justify immediate action even if technically accurate.


---

Alert Fatigue Is the Primary Failure Mode

What it is

Alert fatigue occurs when responders receive so many low-value alerts that acknowledging them becomes habitual.

Eventually meaningful alerts receive the same automatic dismissal.

Why it exists

Human attention adapts.

If ninety-nine alerts require no action, the hundredth is assumed to require none either.

Options

Systems may favor:

high sensitivity

balanced sensitivity

high specificity


Trade-offs

High sensitivity

Pros

Rarely misses problems.


Cons

Many false positives.

Rapid alert fatigue.



---

High specificity

Pros

Most alerts matter.

Higher responder trust.


Cons

Some genuine problems may initially go unnoticed.


When to choose each option

Production paging systems should strongly prefer high specificity.

Missing an occasional non-critical event is often less harmful than training responders to distrust every notification.

Common failure modes

This is the monitoring equivalent of the process failure described in Chapter 49.

The organization begins optimizing for:

> "An alert was generated."



instead of:

> "A human investigated an important problem."



The metric becomes proxy activity rather than operational effectiveness.

Example

A monitoring system that pages every time CPU briefly exceeds 80% may generate hundreds of overnight alerts despite no customer-visible impact.

Eventually operators stop believing that CPU alerts indicate meaningful incidents.


---

Page on Symptoms, Not Every Possible Cause

What it is

Alerts should primarily target symptoms users actually experience rather than every internal condition that could eventually produce one.

Why it exists

Many internal anomalies resolve automatically.

Users never notice them.

Paging humans for every transient condition creates noise without improving reliability.

Options

Symptom-based alerting

Examples:

elevated error rate

elevated latency

request failures

unavailable service



---

Cause-based alerting

Examples:

CPU threshold exceeded

one unhealthy replica

temporary disk utilization spike

garbage collection pause


Trade-offs

Symptom-based

Pros

Closely aligned with user experience.

Fewer false positives.

Higher confidence that intervention is necessary.


Cons

Root cause still requires investigation.

Internal degradation may be detected later.



---

Cause-based

Pros

Earlier warning.

May prevent future incidents.


Cons

Many alerts never affect users.

Higher false-positive rate.

Greater fatigue.


When to choose each option

Default to symptom-based paging.

Cause-based alerts are appropriate when a specific internal condition reliably predicts imminent user-visible failure and cannot recover automatically.

Common failure modes

Teams page on every unhealthy replica in a highly redundant system.

The orchestration platform replaces the failed instance automatically.

Customers never experience an outage.

The alert generated work without preventing harm.

Example

The guidance in Google's Site Reliability Engineering practices consistently favors alerting on observable service symptoms such as elevated request latency or error rate rather than intermediate infrastructure conditions that may self-correct before users notice them.


---

Separate Informational, Operational, and Emergency Signals

What it is

Not every detected condition deserves the same response.

Operational monitoring should distinguish between information, planned work, and emergencies.

Why it exists

Treating every anomaly identically eliminates prioritization.

Responders become unable to distinguish routine maintenance from genuine incidents.

Options

A practical tiering model is:

Log-worthy — record for future investigation.

Dashboard-worthy — monitor operational trends.

Ticket-worthy — investigate during normal working hours.

Page-worthy — immediate human response required.


Trade-offs

Multiple severity levels introduce additional policy decisions.

They also preserve the meaning of urgent notifications by preventing less important conditions from competing for attention.

When to choose each option

Reserve paging for conditions where immediate action materially changes the outcome.

Everything else belongs in a lower tier.

Common failure modes

Organizations flatten severity into a single notification channel.

Routine maintenance warnings, capacity planning reminders, and customer-facing outages all arrive through the same mechanism.

Priority becomes meaningless.

Example

Mainstream incident management platforms such as PagerDuty implement routing, escalation policies, and notification rules around exactly this concept: different classes of operational events follow different escalation paths rather than treating every notification as equally urgent.


---

Escalation Should Reflect Ownership

What it is

Alerts should be routed to the team capable of resolving the problem.

An alert is not complete until ownership is defined.

Why it exists

The fastest possible notification has little value if it reaches the wrong responder.

Operational delay often comes from discovering ownership rather than diagnosing the failure itself.

Options

Organizations commonly route alerts by:

service ownership

infrastructure ownership

business function

primary and secondary on-call rotations


Escalation policies determine what happens if an alert is not acknowledged within an expected period.

Trade-offs

Fine-grained ownership reduces unnecessary interruptions but requires accurate service ownership information.

Broad routing simplifies configuration but increases the number of people disturbed by incidents outside their expertise.

When to choose each option

Route alerts to the smallest group capable of resolving the issue without additional handoffs.

Escalate only when expected response times are exceeded.

Common failure modes

An alert reaches a general operations team that lacks permission or knowledge to resolve the problem.

Time is lost transferring responsibility between teams while the incident continues.

Example

Modern on-call systems typically implement escalation chains that notify progressively broader groups only if the initial responder does not acknowledge the page within a defined interval.


---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

Experienced engineers generally agree that alert fatigue is dangerous. They disagree about how aggressively to avoid it.

One philosophy favors early warning. Its advocates argue that detecting infrastructure anomalies before users notice them provides valuable time to intervene and prevents larger incidents.

The opposing philosophy argues that infrastructure is expected to experience transient failures. Modern systems are deliberately designed to tolerate node failures, brief latency spikes, and automatic retries. Paging on every potential cause undermines confidence in the paging system itself.

The disagreement often reflects differences in system architecture. Highly automated, self-healing systems benefit from symptom-based alerting because many internal failures resolve without human involvement. Simpler systems with little redundancy may reasonably page on infrastructure conditions that almost always precede customer-visible outages.

The consistent principle is that alerts exist to initiate useful human action. Any alert that repeatedly interrupts responders without changing their behavior should be reconsidered, regardless of how accurately it detects technical anomalies.


---

Summary

Alerting is not another observability signal but a decision layered on top of the signals collected in Chapters 69 and 70. Its purpose is to determine when an observation justifies interrupting a human, making human attention the scarce resource that alerting systems must optimize.

The primary design criterion is actionability. Every page should have an expected responder, a clear next step, and ideally a corresponding runbook from Chapter 68. Conditions that merely provide useful information belong on dashboards or in tickets rather than in someone's pocket overnight.

The dominant failure mode is alert fatigue. Excessive low-value notifications train responders to acknowledge alerts reflexively, reducing the effectiveness of the alerts that truly matter. For this reason, production paging systems should generally favor symptom-based alerts that reflect user-visible failures over cause-based alerts that often represent transient, self-correcting infrastructure conditions.

The next chapter turns to the final observability signal introduced in Chapter 70: distributed tracing. While logs preserve events and metrics summarize behavior, traces reconstruct the complete execution path of an individual request across service boundaries, providing the system-wide visibility that no single service possesses on its own.

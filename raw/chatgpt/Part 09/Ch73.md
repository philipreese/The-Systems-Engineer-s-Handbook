Chapter 73 — Error Budgets and SLOs

Purpose

Every observability system ultimately exists to answer one question:

"Is the system meeting the level of reliability we intended?"

Without a defined target, logs, metrics, traces, and alerts become measurements without purpose. Engineers can observe latency increasing, error rates fluctuating, or services failing, but they cannot determine whether those observations represent acceptable variation or operational failure.

This chapter closes Part IX by defining that target. It fulfills the forward references from Chapters 07, 62, and 68 by introducing Service Level Indicators (SLIs), Service Level Objectives (SLOs), Service Level Agreements (SLAs), and error budgets. Together, they transform reliability from a subjective aspiration into a measurable engineering objective.

This also returns to the optimization framework established in Chapter 01. Once reliability has an explicit target, engineering effort beyond that target becomes a trade-off rather than an unquestioned virtue.

Key Concepts

The central ideas introduced here are:

Reliability requires measurable objectives.

SLIs measure system behavior.

SLOs define internal engineering targets.

SLAs define external commitments.

Every SLO creates an explicit error budget.

Error budgets are intended to be spent deliberately, not preserved indefinitely.



---

SLI, SLO, and SLA Describe Different Things

What it is

Three closely related terms are frequently confused.

They describe different layers of the same reliability model.

Service Level Indicator (SLI) — the measured value.

Service Level Objective (SLO) — the internal target for that measurement.

Service Level Agreement (SLA) — the external commitment made to customers.


Why it exists

Without separating measurement, objectives, and contractual promises, organizations struggle to reason about reliability.

Engineering targets become contractual obligations.

Operational metrics become business commitments.

The distinctions exist to prevent those misunderstandings.

Options

An availability objective might be expressed as:

SLI: Percentage of successful requests.

SLO: 99.95% monthly availability.

SLA: 99.9% monthly availability guaranteed to customers.


The indicator measures.

The objective guides engineering.

The agreement governs customer expectations.

Trade-offs

Maintaining separate internal and external targets creates operational margin.

Using identical SLOs and SLAs eliminates that margin, making every internal miss immediately visible as a contractual failure.

When to choose each option

SLIs should measure behavior users actually experience.

SLOs should reflect the level of reliability the engineering organization intends to deliver.

SLAs should represent promises the organization is prepared to uphold commercially.

Common failure modes

Organizations often refer to "our SLA" when discussing internal operational targets.

The result is confusion about whether a missed target represents an engineering issue or a contractual obligation.

Example

Major cloud providers commonly publish uptime SLAs that are intentionally less stringent than the internal objectives used to operate their services. That operational margin allows engineering teams to respond before customer commitments are violated.


---

An SLO Defines "Reliable Enough"

What it is

An SLO specifies the level of service the system is expected to achieve over a defined period.

Examples include:

99.9% successful requests per month

99% of requests complete within 300 ms

99.95% monthly availability


Why it exists

Without explicit objectives, reliability discussions become subjective.

One engineer considers a brief outage acceptable.

Another considers it catastrophic.

An SLO replaces opinion with an agreed target.

Options

Objectives may focus on:

availability

latency

correctness

freshness

durability


The appropriate indicator depends on the service.

Trade-offs

Higher objectives improve customer experience but require increasing engineering investment.

Lower objectives reduce operational cost but permit greater user-visible failure.

When to choose each option

Objectives should reflect business requirements rather than engineering ambition.

Not every internal service requires five nines of availability.

Not every customer-facing API can tolerate ninety-five percent uptime.

Common failure modes

Teams select impressive-looking numbers without understanding their operational consequences.

The objective becomes aspirational rather than actionable.

Example

An online banking system and an internal reporting dashboard may both measure availability, yet reasonably adopt different SLOs because the consequences of downtime differ substantially.


---

Every SLO Creates an Error Budget

What it is

An error budget is the amount of failure permitted by an SLO.

It is not an additional policy.

It follows directly from the objective.

For example:

99.9% availability permits approximately 0.1% unavailability.

Across a thirty-day month, that corresponds to roughly forty-three minutes of downtime.


Why it exists

No distributed system achieves perfect reliability indefinitely.

An error budget acknowledges this reality explicitly.

Instead of pretending failures should never occur, it defines how much failure is acceptable.

Options

Organizations may:

ignore the budget

attempt never to consume it

manage it deliberately


Trade-offs

Treating every failure as unacceptable discourages change and experimentation.

Treating the budget as meaningless removes operational discipline.

Managing it deliberately balances reliability against development velocity.

When to choose each option

The budget should be viewed as an engineering resource.

If reliability comfortably exceeds the objective, the organization can reasonably accept additional operational risk in pursuit of other goals.

Common failure modes

Teams celebrate months with zero budget consumption regardless of context.

This often indicates that the organization avoided valuable improvements rather than achieving an optimal balance.

Example

Google's Site Reliability Engineering practices introduced the idea of treating the error budget as a spendable engineering resource rather than a failure to be avoided at all costs.


---

Error Budgets Exist to Be Spent Deliberately

What it is

The purpose of an error budget is not merely to measure reliability.

Its purpose is to guide engineering decisions.

Why it exists

Every deployment, migration, architectural change, or operational experiment carries some risk.

The error budget defines how much of that risk the organization has already agreed is acceptable.

Options

Organizations may spend error budget on:

production deployments

infrastructure migrations

architectural refactoring

operational experiments

controlled feature rollouts


Trade-offs

Using the budget accelerates development but increases short-term operational risk.

Avoiding all budget consumption maximizes reliability but may slow product evolution unnecessarily.

When to choose each option

If substantial budget remains, accepting measured operational risk is often justified.

If the budget is nearly exhausted, reliability improvements should generally take priority over additional risk.

Common failure modes

Organizations pursue ever-higher availability despite already exceeding their stated objective.

Engineering effort shifts toward eliminating increasingly rare failures that produce little practical benefit.

This is Principle 6 applied to reliability.

Once the agreed objective is consistently met, additional reliability investment must justify its opportunity cost against other engineering work.

Example

A service consistently achieving 99.99% availability despite a 99.9% objective may reasonably prioritize new capabilities over additional availability work unless business requirements change.


---

Error Budgets Connect Observability to Deployment

What it is

Observability produces measurements.

Error budgets determine whether those measurements permit additional operational risk.

Why it exists

Chapter 62 introduced canary deployments and progressive rollout.

Those mechanisms require an objective criterion for deciding whether deployment should continue.

Error budget consumption provides that criterion.

Options

Deployment decisions may consider:

current budget consumption

recent burn rate

remaining monthly budget

historical reliability trends


Trade-offs

Strict gating reduces the likelihood of worsening an already unstable system.

Relaxed gating increases deployment velocity but may exhaust the available reliability margin.

When to choose each option

When error budget is being consumed unusually rapidly, deployment systems should become increasingly conservative.

The deployment mechanics themselves remain the responsibility of Chapter 62.

Common failure modes

Organizations continue deploying aggressively despite rapidly increasing operational failures.

The deployment system measures technical success while ignoring deteriorating customer experience.

Example

Many progressive deployment systems halt further rollout automatically when observed reliability indicates that continuing deployment would consume error budget at an unacceptable rate.


---

Error Budgets Connect Observability to Operations

What it is

Runbooks and incident response ultimately exist to preserve the remaining error budget.

Why it exists

Chapter 68 described how responders recover systems once alerted.

The operational purpose of that recovery is to reduce ongoing reliability loss.

Every minute of unresolved outage consumes additional budget.

Options

Organizations may:

tolerate ongoing degradation

investigate manually

execute documented recovery procedures


Trade-offs

Well-designed operational procedures reduce MTTR and therefore reduce budget consumption.

Poor operational response extends outages and consumes budget unnecessarily.

When to choose each option

Whenever operational failures begin spending error budget unexpectedly, rapid recovery becomes more valuable than further diagnosis.

Detailed recovery procedures remain the subject of Chapter 68.

Common failure modes

Teams optimize incident documentation independently of operational objectives.

The documentation becomes an end in itself rather than a tool for reducing customer impact.

Example

A responder following a database failover runbook is not merely restoring service—they are slowing the rate at which the remaining monthly error budget is being consumed.


---

Burn Rate Measures How Quickly the Budget Is Being Spent

What it is

Burn rate measures the speed at which an error budget is being consumed relative to its expected rate.

A brief outage can consume days or weeks of allowable failure within minutes.

Why it exists

The remaining budget alone does not indicate urgency.

A system spending its budget gradually presents a different operational situation from one consuming it rapidly.

Options

Organizations commonly evaluate:

short-term burn rate

long-term burn rate

combinations of both


Trade-offs

Short observation windows detect sudden incidents quickly but react to transient fluctuations.

Long observation windows provide stability but respond more slowly.

Combining multiple windows balances sensitivity and stability.

When to choose each option

Large production systems commonly evaluate both short- and long-term burn simultaneously to distinguish sustained incidents from temporary noise.

The alert routing decisions themselves remain the responsibility of Chapter 71.

Common failure modes

Teams alert on instantaneous error rate without considering whether the resulting reliability loss meaningfully threatens the objective.

This creates unnecessary operational noise.

Example

Google's SRE Workbook describes multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting as a practical mechanism for identifying incidents that are consuming the error budget rapidly enough to justify immediate operational response.


---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

Experienced engineers generally agree that reliability should be measured. They disagree about where objectives should be set.

One philosophy argues for ambitious targets whenever technically achievable. Higher reliability improves customer trust and reduces operational disruption, so exceeding current objectives is viewed as a worthwhile investment.

The opposing philosophy argues that reliability is one engineering objective among many. Time spent increasing availability from 99.95% to 99.99% is time unavailable for new features, security improvements, performance work, or technical debt reduction. Once an agreed objective is consistently achieved, additional reliability work must compete with every other engineering investment.

The disagreement is rarely about mathematics. It is about opportunity cost. Error budgets provide a common framework by making that trade-off explicit instead of allowing reliability to become an unlimited optimization target.


---

Summary

Service level indicators, objectives, and agreements define distinct layers of reliability. SLIs measure user-visible behavior, SLOs establish internal engineering targets for those measurements, and SLAs communicate external commitments that are typically set somewhat lower to preserve operational margin.

Every SLO creates an error budget: an explicit allowance for acceptable failure over a defined period. Rather than treating any consumption of that budget as undesirable, effective engineering organizations manage it deliberately, spending reliability margin on deployments, migrations, and experimentation while slowing operational risk when the remaining budget becomes scarce. This reframes reliability as a resource to allocate rather than an objective to maximize indefinitely.

Error budgets also connect the rest of Part IX into a coherent system. The logs, metrics, and traces collected in Chapters 69 through 72 provide the measurements that produce SLIs. Chapter 71's alerting decisions determine when error budget is being consumed quickly enough to require immediate human attention. Chapter 62's deployment mechanisms use that information to decide whether progressive rollouts should continue or stop, while Chapter 68's runbooks exist to restore service quickly enough to preserve the remaining budget.

With this chapter, Part IX reaches the same conclusion as Chapter 01: optimization requires an explicit target. Observability is valuable not because systems can be measured, but because those measurements are tied to defined operational objectives that guide engineering decisions.

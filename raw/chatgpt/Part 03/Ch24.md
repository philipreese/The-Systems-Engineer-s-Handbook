Chapter 24 — Authentication and Authorization Boundaries

1. Purpose

Every API has to answer two questions before it does anything useful:

Who is this caller?

Are they allowed to do this?


This chapter is not about cryptographic protocols or identity systems. It is about where those decisions are enforced in a distributed system, and what architectural shape that enforcement creates.

The placement of authentication and authorization logic determines whether your system behaves like a single trusted machine or a collection of independently secured components.


---

2. Prerequisites

Assumed:

API boundary exists and is already defined (Ch 15–23)

Service decomposition and distributed execution are in place (Part II context)

Requests cross trust boundaries and may traverse multiple services


Not covered:

Cryptographic details of OAuth, JWT, or signing mechanisms (Part XI)

3. Key Concepts

Authentication (authn): identity of the caller

Authorization (authz): what the caller is permitted to do

Enforcement boundary placement: edge vs per-service

Token propagation vs revalidation

Zero-trust vs perimeter-trust models

Confused-deputy as a systemic failure mode



---

Authentication vs Authorization at the Boundary

Identity vs Permission as Separate Layers

What it is: Authentication establishes identity (“who”), authorization enforces permissions (“what they can do”).

Why it exists: Separating identity from permission allows systems to evolve access policies independently of login mechanisms.

Options:

Coupled authn/authz at a single boundary layer

Authn at edge, authz in services

Fully distributed authn/authz per service


Trade-offs:

Coupled: simple mental model, centralized control


Separated: flexible policy enforcement, better scalability


− Coupled: hard to evolve permissions without touching auth system

− Fully distributed: inconsistent enforcement, duplication risk


When to choose each:

Coupled: small systems or early-stage architectures

Edge authn + service authz: most modern distributed systems

Fully distributed: rare, usually legacy or highly autonomous systems


Common failure modes:

Treating authentication as sufficient for authorization

Duplicating inconsistent authorization logic across services

Assuming identity implies permission in downstream services


Example:

OAuth-based systems authenticate at gateway, but each service enforces its own resource-level authorization rules

Large platforms (Google, AWS-style systems) separate identity issuance from per-service permission checks



---

Where Enforcement Happens

Centralized Gateway vs Distributed Enforcement

What it is: Authentication and sometimes authorization can be enforced at:

API gateway (centralized control point)

Each individual service (distributed enforcement)

Hybrid model (gateway + service-level checks)


Why it exists: Every system must decide whether trust is centralized or distributed across services.

Options:

Gateway-only enforcement

Service-level enforcement

Dual-layer enforcement (gateway + service)


Trade-offs:

Gateway-only: consistent policy, single control plane


Service-level: fine-grained control, avoids blind trust in network


Dual-layer: defense-in-depth, reduces single point of failure


− Gateway-only: creates implicit trust boundary inside network

− Service-level: duplicated logic, inconsistent enforcement risk

− Dual-layer: complexity and potential performance overhead


When to choose each:

Gateway-only: simple systems, strong internal trust assumptions

Service-level: microservices with independent ownership boundaries

Dual-layer: large-scale systems with high security requirements (common in production at scale)


Common failure modes:

Assuming gateway enforcement makes internal services safe by default

Forgetting to enforce authz in one service, creating a bypass path

Diverging policy implementations across services

Overloading gateway with business logic it should not own


Example:

Kong or Envoy enforcing authentication at the edge

Internal services in Kubernetes still performing authorization checks independently

AWS services often enforce both IAM at edge and resource-level policies internally

Token Propagation and Trust Boundaries

Carrying Identity Through the System

What it is: A token (often a signed identity assertion) is passed from service to service, carrying authenticated identity context across the system.

Why it exists: To avoid re-authenticating users at every hop while preserving identity context across distributed calls.

Options:

Edge-issued token propagated downstream (JWT-style)

Token exchanged per hop (delegated tokens)

No propagation (re-authenticate or re-map identity at each service)

Trusted internal network (no verification after ingress)


Trade-offs:

Propagation: scalable identity continuity, low overhead


Per-hop verification: stronger security, reduces trust assumptions


− Propagation without verification: risk of impersonation if trust boundary is broken

− No propagation: high overhead, brittle identity reconstruction


When to choose each:

Token propagation: default in modern distributed systems

Per-hop verification: zero-trust architectures

No propagation: legacy monolith-to-service transitions

Trusted network: only acceptable in tightly controlled environments (increasingly rare)


Common failure modes:

Blindly trusting internal network traffic as authenticated

Treating tokens as opaque data instead of verified assertions

Passing privileged tokens downstream without scoping reduction

Failing to enforce audience restrictions on tokens


Example:

OAuth 2.0 / OpenID Connect issues tokens that are propagated across services

Microservice architectures using JWTs validate tokens at each service boundary

Some internal platforms rely on mTLS + token propagation for layered identity assurance

Confused Deputy Problem

When Trust Boundaries Collapse Internally

What it is: A service performs an action on behalf of a caller using its own elevated privileges, but incorrectly trusts that the caller is authorized to do so — effectively becoming an unwitting proxy for abuse.

Why it exists: Because internal service-to-service trust often replaces explicit authorization checks with network-level trust assumptions.

Options:

Strict per-service authorization checks

Implicit trust within network perimeter

Hybrid zero-trust model with scoped delegation


Trade-offs:

Strict checks: prevents lateral abuse across services


Perimeter trust: simpler internal communication model


− Perimeter trust: vulnerable to internal compromise or misrouting

− Strict checks: more overhead and policy complexity


When to choose each:

Strict checks: systems with sensitive operations or multi-tenant exposure

Perimeter trust: small, isolated systems with controlled deployment environments

Hybrid: most large-scale modern systems


Common failure modes:

A compromised or buggy service issuing unauthorized internal requests

A service acting on behalf of a user without verifying delegated permission scope

Internal APIs assuming upstream validation is sufficient

Privilege escalation through internal routing paths


Example:

Internal microservice uses admin credentials because it “trusts” upstream gateway validation

A service accidentally exposes internal endpoints that bypass user-level authorization

Classic confused deputy scenario in distributed systems where a trusted component is tricked into misusing its own authority



---

Zero Trust as Architectural Response

Verify Every Hop, Not Just the Edge

What it is: A model where no request is trusted based solely on network location; identity and authorization are re-validated at every service boundary.

Why it exists: Because network perimeter assumptions break down in distributed systems where internal services are independently deployable and potentially compromised.

Options:

Perimeter security model (trust internal network)

Zero-trust model (verify each request independently)

Hybrid layered trust (partial verification at multiple boundaries)


Trade-offs:

Zero trust: strong isolation between services, limits blast radius


Perimeter trust: lower overhead, simpler architecture


− Zero trust: more latency and policy complexity

− Perimeter trust: large blast radius if internal trust is breached


When to choose each:

Zero trust: modern microservices, multi-tenant systems, security-critical platforms

Perimeter trust: tightly controlled monoliths or isolated internal systems

Hybrid: most real-world production systems (edge + internal validation mix)


Common failure modes:

Assuming “inside the VPC” equals safe

Not revalidating tokens at service boundaries

Allowing privilege escalation through internal calls

Treating zero trust as optional documentation rather than enforced architecture


Example:

Google’s internal infrastructure uses strong per-service identity verification

Modern service meshes (Envoy-based systems) enforce mTLS and identity at each hop

Legacy internal systems often relied on flat trusted networks and suffered from lateral movement risk



---

Why Smart Engineers Disagree

The disagreement is fundamentally about trust assumptions.

Perimeter-trust engineers argue:

Internal networks are controlled environments

Centralized auth at the gateway is sufficient

Per-service auth adds unnecessary complexity


Zero-trust engineers argue:

Internal networks are not reliable security boundaries

Services fail, get compromised, or misconfigured

Every hop must independently enforce correctness


Platform engineers take a hybrid stance:

Centralized authentication for consistency

Distributed authorization for correctness and isolation



The real divide is:

Trust the network boundary vs

Trust only explicit, verifiable identity at each interaction


Modern large-scale systems overwhelmingly converge toward the second view.


---

Closing Structural Insight

Authentication and authorization are not just “security features.” They define the shape of trust in your system.

Where you enforce them determines whether your architecture behaves like:

a single trusted machine split into parts, or

a network of mutually distrustful components that must explicitly prove every request.


Everything downstream — API design, service decomposition, and failure isolation — inherits that decision.
